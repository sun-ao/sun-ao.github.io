Why Build Your Own PC?
#

In an era where pre-built PCs and laptops are everywhere, building your own computer might seem like unnecessary effort. However, DIY PC building still offers irreplaceable advantages:

Better Value for Money. With the same budget, a self-assembled PC often delivers better performance than branded machines. Pre-built PCs need to cover R&D, marketing, and after-sales costs, while DIY only pays for the hardware itself.

Fully Customizable. You can configure precisely according to personal needs—gamers can allocate more budget to the graphics card, content creators can prioritize CPU and memory, and storage enthusiasts can fill up drive bays. No more “one-size-fits-all” from branded machines.

Transparent and Controllable. Every component’s model and specifications are crystal clear. No need to worry about common “cut corners” in pre-built PCs—like overstated power supply ratings, budget motherboards, or inadequate cooling.

Fun and Growth. Assembling a computer with your own hands, understanding the role of each component—this sense of achievement and accumulated knowledge cannot be obtained by simply buying one. When minor issues arise, you’ll be better equipped to troubleshoot and solve them yourself.

Of course, DIY PC building has its barriers: you need to learn hardware knowledge, spend time comparing prices, and do the assembly yourself. The goal of this article is to help you overcome these barriers.

Core Components Explained
#

A computer consists of multiple hardware components working together, each with its specific role. Understanding the function and selection criteria of these components is the foundation of PC configuration.

CPU: The Brain of the Computer
#

CPU (Central Processing Unit) is the core of the computer, responsible for executing various calculations and instructions. Think of it as the “commander-in-chief” of a factory, determining the processing speed and efficiency of the entire computer.

Key Parameters Explained
#

Core Count and Thread Count. The number of cores determines how many independent tasks the CPU can process simultaneously, while thread count is the logical parallel processing capability. Modern CPUs generally support hyper-threading technology (Intel calls it Hyper-Threading, AMD calls it SMT), where one physical core can simulate two logical threads.

An analogy: core count is the number of workshops in a factory, thread count is the number of workers. 4 cores and 8 threads means 4 workshops with 8 workers, each workshop having 2 workers working in shifts.

For daily office work and light gaming, 4-6 cores are sufficient; for gaming with streaming or video editing, 8+ cores are recommended; for professional rendering, compiling, and other heavy tasks, consider 12-16 cores or more.

Base Clock and Boost Clock. The base clock is the CPU’s fundamental operating frequency, while boost clock is the maximum frequency automatically achieved under high load. Higher frequencies mean stronger single-core performance.

Note that CPUs from different generations and architectures cannot be compared purely by frequency. A 3.0GHz CPU with new architecture might be faster than a 4.0GHz CPU with old architecture—just like a new 2.0T engine might outperform an old 3.0L engine.

Cache. CPU cache is high-speed storage located inside the CPU, divided into L1, L2, and L3 levels. Larger cache means higher hit rate when the CPU accesses data, resulting in better performance. L3 cache has a significant impact on gaming performance.

Manufacturing Process. Smaller process numbers mean more densely packed transistors and better energy efficiency. Currently, 5nm and 7nm processes are mainstream. CPUs with advanced processes typically have lower heat and power consumption.

#

Both camps have their advantages:

Selection advice: Gamers should prioritize single-core performance and cache; content creators should prioritize multi-core performance. Check the latest CPU hierarchy charts and choose based on budget and needs.

Selection Strategy
#

  1. Define Your Purpose. Office and home use: entry-level (i3/R3 or i5/R5); Gaming: mainstream (i5/R5); Professional work: mid-to-high-end (i7/R7 or above).

  2. Generation Over Positioning. i5-13600K beats i7-11700K because generational gaps often outweigh positioning differences. “Buy new, not old” is an important principle for CPU selection.

  3. Pay Attention to Suffixes. Intel: K=unlocked, F=no integrated graphics, KF=unlocked without iGPU; AMD: X=enhanced, 3D=stacked cache (gaming optimized). Choose based on needs—don’t pay for features you won’t use.

  4. Tray vs Boxed. Tray CPUs are cheaper but have no official warranty; boxed CPUs cost more but have complete warranty. Beginners should choose boxed; experienced builders can consider tray.

Graphics Card: The Core of Graphics Processing
#

GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) handles graphics rendering tasks and is one of the most watched components for gamers and content creators.

Key Parameters Explained
#

VRAM Capacity and Bus Width. VRAM is the graphics card’s “memory,” storing graphics data to be processed. VRAM capacity determines how complex scenes can be handled, while bus width determines data transfer speed.

4GB VRAM is suitable for 1080P light gaming, 8GB for 1080P/2K mainstream gaming, 12GB+ for 4K gaming or professional rendering. 128-bit bus width is entry-level, 192-256-bit is mainstream, 384-bit+ is high-end.

CUDA Cores / Stream Processors. These are the “computing units” of the graphics card—the more there are, the stronger the parallel computing capability. NVIDIA calls them CUDA cores, AMD calls them stream processors. Note that the two architectures differ, so numbers cannot be directly compared.

VRAM Type. GDDR6 is mainstream, GDDR6X is faster, HBM/HBM2 is for top-tier professional cards. Higher VRAM frequency is better.

Ray Tracing and DLSS/FSR. Ray tracing is a new generation rendering technology that presents more realistic lighting and shadow effects. DLSS (NVIDIA) and FSR (AMD) are upscaling technologies that can significantly boost frame rates with minimal quality loss.

NVIDIA vs AMD: How to Choose?
#

FeatureNVIDIAAMD
Ray Tracing PerformanceLeading, mature hardware accelerationSupported but significant performance loss
UpscalingDLSS has better resultsFSR is open and universal
ProductivityCUDA ecosystem complete, strong AI accelerationSuitable for pure gaming scenarios
PriceHigher at same tierBetter value

Selection advice: For ray tracing and AI features, choose NVIDIA; for pure gaming value, choose AMD; for professional productivity, prioritize NVIDIA (CUDA ecosystem).

Pitfall Avoidance Guide
#

  1. Beware of “Mining Cards”. After the cryptocurrency boom, many used graphics cards flooded the market. Mining cards ran at high load for long periods and have questionable lifespan. Beginners should buy new; buying used requires discrimination skills.

  2. Look at Model, Not Just VRAM. GT 1030 4GB is worse than GTX 1650 4GB because the core performance gap is huge. VRAM is not the only indicator—core performance matters more.

  3. Note Power Supply Requirements. High-end graphics cards consume a lot of power—RTX 4090 even needs 450W+. When selecting a graphics card, consider power supply capacity simultaneously.

  4. Brand Differences. The same chip from different brands has different build quality, cooling, and warranty. Top-tier brands (ASUS, MSI, Gigabyte, etc.) have more reliable quality; second and third-tier brands offer better value.

Memory: The Temporary Workspace for Data
#

RAM (Random Access Memory) is the bridge between CPU and storage, holding data and programs currently in use. More memory means more programs can run simultaneously; faster memory means higher data read/write efficiency.

Key Parameters Explained
#

Capacity. 8GB is the minimum threshold, barely enough for daily office work; 16GB is the mainstream recommendation, handling gaming and light creation; 32GB is suitable for heavy multi-tasking and professional creation; 64GB+ is for professional workstations.

Frequency. Memory frequency is measured in MHz—higher frequency means faster speed. DDR4 mainstream frequency is 2666-3600MHz, DDR5 mainstream is 4800-6400MHz. However, frequency improvements have diminishing returns, so don’t blindly pursue high frequencies.

Timing. Timing represents the latency of memory accessing data, commonly formatted as CL16-18-18-38. Lower timing is better at the same frequency, with CL being the most important indicator. CL16-18 is excellent for DDR4, CL30-40 is mainstream for DDR5.

Generation. DDR4 and DDR5 are currently mainstream. DDR5 has higher frequency and lower power consumption but also higher price. When choosing, note which generation your motherboard supports—they are not compatible.

The Importance of Dual Channel
#

Dual channel means using two memory sticks, allowing the memory controller to read and write data through two channels simultaneously, theoretically doubling bandwidth.

An analogy: single channel is one lane, dual channel is two lanes—more vehicles can pass in the same time.

For users with integrated graphics, dual channel is especially important—integrated graphics use system memory as VRAM, and dual channel significantly improves graphics performance. For discrete graphics users, dual channel also provides 10-20% improvement in gaming frame rates.

Practical advice: For 16GB memory, choose 2×8GB instead of 1×16GB; for 32GB, choose 2×16GB or 4×8GB.

Selection Strategy
#

  1. Capacity Before Frequency. Insufficient capacity is more painful than low frequency—prioritize capacity.

  2. Match Frequency to CPU. CPUs have upper limits on supported memory frequency; exceeding the limit will downclock. For example, if CPU supports DDR4 3200, buying DDR4 3600 will downclock to 3200.

  3. Brand Selection. Top-tier brands like Kingston, G.Skill, ADATA, and Corsair offer stable quality; domestic brands like Gloway and Asgard offer good value. Regular users don’t need to pursue “gaming heatsinks”—practicality is key.

  4. Note Compatibility. Some motherboards have limited support for high-frequency memory; check the motherboard’s memory compatibility list (QVL) before purchasing.

Storage: The Permanent Warehouse for Data
#

Storage is where the operating system, software, and files are kept. Unlike memory, data in storage doesn’t disappear when power is off.

SSD vs HDD
#

SSD (Solid State Drive) uses flash memory chips to store data—extremely fast, silent, and shock-resistant. It’s the current mainstream choice.

HDD (Hard Disk Drive) uses magnetic platters to store data—slow, noisy, and sensitive to vibration, but cheap with large capacity and relatively easy data recovery.

Current build configurations typically use SSD as the system drive and frequently used software drive, with HDD as large-capacity storage (if needed).

NVMe vs SATA
#

These are two interface types for SSDs.

NVMe SSD uses the M.2 interface and transfers data through PCIe channels—extremely fast. PCIe 3.0 x4 sequential read speeds can reach 3500MB/s, PCIe 4.0 can reach 7000MB/s, and PCIe 5.0 breaks 10000MB/s.

SATA SSD uses the SATA interface with a speed limit of about 550MB/s, but has good compatibility and low price.

Selection advice: System drive and frequently used software drive should be NVMe SSD; large-capacity storage can be SATA SSD or HDD.

Capacity Planning
#

  • 256GB: Only for OS and a few programs, not recommended
  • 512GB: Entry-level choice, OS + common software + a few games
  • 1TB: Mainstream recommendation, OS + software + games + files, ample space
  • 2TB+: Ideal for gamers and creators, no need to worry about space

Selection Strategy
#

  1. Look at Controller and NAND. The controller is the SSD’s “CPU,” affecting performance and lifespan; NAND determines capacity and durability. Original NAND (Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron, WD/SanDisk, Kioxia) has the best quality.

  2. Pay Attention to TBW. TBW (Terabytes Written) indicates the total amount of data that can be written during the warranty period—higher numbers mean longer lifespan. Mainstream 1TB SSDs have TBW around 600.

  3. Performance Tiers. Entry-level (no cache) suitable for regular users; mainstream (with cache) suitable for gaming and creation; flagship (high-end controller + cache) suitable for enthusiasts.

  4. Brand Recommendations. Top-tier: Samsung, WD, SK Hynix, Kioxia; Value: ZHITAI, Gloway, Kingston.

Secondary Components Overview
#

Motherboard: The Hub Connecting Everything
#

The motherboard is the carrier for all hardware, responsible for connecting and coordinating all components.

Chipset determines the motherboard’s expansion capabilities and features. Intel mainstream chipsets: H610 (entry), B760 (mainstream), Z790 (high-end); AMD mainstream chipsets: A620 (entry), B650 (mainstream), X670 (high-end).

Power Phases affect CPU stability and overclocking potential. High-end CPUs need more power phases; entry motherboards might not handle them.

Expansion Interfaces include M.2 slot count, SATA port count, PCIe slots, etc. Choose based on future upgrade needs.

Selection advice: Motherboard must match CPU socket, power delivery must meet CPU needs, expansion interfaces should consider future upgrades. No need to pursue top-tier motherboards—sufficient is best.

Power Supply: The Energy Source for the System
#

The power supply provides electricity to all components, and its quality directly affects system stability.

Power Calculation. Estimate total power based on CPU and graphics card consumption, with 30% headroom recommended. For example: CPU 65W + GPU 200W + Others 50W = 315W, recommend 450W+ power supply.

Certification Level. 80 Plus certification indicates conversion efficiency, from low to high: White, Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum, Titanium. Gold and above offer the best value.

Modularity Type. Full modular (all cables detachable) for clean cable management; semi-modular (some cables detachable) for value; non-modular (fixed cables) for lowest price.

Selection advice: Leave headroom for power, choose Gold certification or above, select top-tier brands (Seasonic, Super Flower, FSP, etc.) or reliable second-tier. Power supply is a common area to cut costs, but it’s the last place you should.

Case: The Home for Hardware
#

The case houses all hardware and affects cooling, noise, and aesthetics.

Airflow Design. Mainstream is front-in rear-out, bottom-in top-out airflow. More fan mounting positions mean greater cooling potential.

Compatibility. Case must fit motherboard size (ATX/mATX/ITX), graphics card length, cooler height, and power supply length.

Cable Management Space. Adequate space behind the motherboard tray makes cable management easier, benefiting cooling and aesthetics.

Selection advice: Determine motherboard size and graphics card length first, then choose case. Pay attention to airflow design and build quality—no need to pursue flashy appearances.

CPU Cooler: The Guardian of CPU Temperature
#

CPUs generate significant heat during operation and need coolers to dissipate it.

Air Coolers dissipate heat through metal fins and fans—simple structure, high reliability. Entry-level (under $15) suitable for low-power CPUs; mainstream ($15-40) for mid-range CPUs; high-end dual-tower ($40+) can handle high-end CPUs.

All-in-One Liquid Coolers dissipate heat through pump, radiator, and coolant circulation—higher cooling efficiency but with minimal leak risk. 120mm AIO underperforms similarly priced air coolers; 240mm suits mid-to-high-end CPUs; 360mm suits top-tier CPUs.

Selection advice: Regular users should choose air cooling—better value and reliability; those pursuing extreme overclocking or preferring AIO aesthetics can choose liquid cooling. Note that cooler height must be compatible with the case.

Assembly Process and Tips
#

Once all hardware arrives, assembly can begin. Here’s the basic process:

Preparation
#

  1. Prepare Tools: Phillips screwdriver (magnetic tip preferred), zip ties, work surface
  2. Read the Manual: Motherboard manual is most important—understand port locations and jumper definitions
  3. Anti-static: Touch metal objects to discharge static, or wear anti-static wrist strap

Assembly Steps
#

Step 1: Install CPU

Open the CPU socket retention lever on the motherboard, confirm CPU and socket alignment notches match, gently place the CPU, close the retention lever. Don’t force it—the CPU should drop in naturally.

Step 2: Install Memory

Confirm memory slot locations; for dual channel, use slots 2 and 4. Open memory clips, align with notches, press down evenly until you hear a “click” indicating proper installation.

Step 3: Install M.2 SSD

Find the M.2 slot on the motherboard, insert the SSD, tighten the mounting screw. Some motherboards have heatsinks for M.2 slots—remember to install them.

Step 4: Install CPU Cooler

Apply thermal paste (if cooler doesn’t have pre-applied paste), install cooler mounting bracket, secure the cooler, connect fan power cable to motherboard CPU_FAN header.

Step 5: Install Motherboard in Case

Install motherboard standoffs in the case (positions matching motherboard holes), place motherboard in case, align with standoff holes, tighten screws. Don’t fully tighten at once—pre-tighten then gradually tighten all.

Step 6: Install Power Supply

Place power supply in the PSU bay, fan orientation depends on case design, tighten mounting screws.

Step 7: Install Graphics Card

Remove corresponding case slot covers, insert graphics card into motherboard PCIe slot until you hear the clip lock, tighten graphics card mounting screws. Connect graphics card power cables (if needed).

Step 8: Connect Cables

  • Motherboard power: 24pin large connector
  • CPU power: 4pin or 8pin, usually at motherboard top-left
  • Graphics card power: 6pin or 8pin, connect to graphics card
  • Front panel: Power switch, reset, LEDs, USB, audio—connect according to motherboard manual

Step 9: Cable Management

Use zip ties to organize cables neatly, avoid blocking airflow. Cases with good cable management space can hide cables behind the motherboard tray.

Step 10: Check and Power On

Check all cable connections are secure, confirm no issues, connect power, press power button. First boot may require entering BIOS to set boot order and XMP/EXPO memory overclocking.

Common Issues and Pitfall Avoidance
#

Beginner Mistakes
#

  1. CPU Installed Backwards. CPU and socket have alignment features—if it doesn’t fit, don’t force it. Check orientation.

  2. Memory Not Fully Seated. Improperly installed memory causes boot failure. Ensure both clips are locked.

  3. Forgot CPU Power Connector. Common cause of boot failure. Both motherboard 24pin and CPU power must be connected.

  4. Too Much Thermal Paste. Only a thin layer is needed—too much actually hurts cooling and can overflow causing shorts.

  5. Front Panel Jumpers Wrong. Incorrect switch/reset jumpers cause buttons not to work. Carefully follow the manual.

Pitfall Avoidance Guide
#

  1. Don’t Blindly Pursue High Specs. Hardware updates quickly—sufficient is good enough. Over-specification is wasteful.

  2. Don’t Ignore Power Supply Quality. Poor quality power supplies can damage hardware. Power supply is one of the most worth-investing components.

  3. Don’t Look at Single Parameters Only. CPU isn’t just about core count, GPU isn’t just about VRAM—evaluate comprehensively.

  4. Don’t Ignore Compatibility. CPU and motherboard socket, memory generation, case dimensions all need to match.

  5. Don’t Ignore After-sales. Choose brands and channels with complete after-sales support so you have protection when issues arise.

Conclusion
#

DIY PC building may seem complex, but with the core methodology mastered, you can avoid pitfalls and assemble a satisfactory system.

Key Takeaways
#

CPU Selection: Define purpose, buy new not old, focus on core count and single-core performance.

Graphics Card Selection: Choose NVIDIA or AMD based on needs, beware of mining cards, match power supply.

Memory Selection: Capacity first, dual channel essential, match frequency to CPU.

Storage Selection: NVMe SSD primary, 1TB minimum, focus on original NAND.

Other Components: Power supply quality is most important, motherboard power delivery must be sufficient, cooler must match CPU.

Assembly Points: Read the manual, note alignment features, secure cable connections, organize neatly.

Final Advice
#

DIY PC building is a learning process. Don’t be afraid of mistakes—every DIY enthusiast started as a beginner. When encountering problems, make good use of search engines and community resources. You’ll find that the problem-solving process is also part of the fun.

When you press the power button and see the screen light up, all the effort is worth it. This is not just a computer—it’s a work you built with your own hands.

Happy building!
